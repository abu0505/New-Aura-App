### 1. Mathematics for Data Science I

This subject focuses on foundational algebra and discrete math. If you haven't touched math in a while, this is the first place to focus your energy.

* **Week 1 (Set Theory & Functions):** Number systems, sets and their operations, relations and their types, functions (injective, surjective, bijective).
    * **Exact Resource:** Go to Khan Academy (Free). Access their *Precalculus* course. Focus specifically on the "Composite and inverse functions" module to learn how functions interact.


* **Week 2 (Straight Lines):** Rectangular coordinate systems, calculating slope, parallel and perpendicular lines, point-slope and general representations of a line, straight-line fitting.
    * **Exact Resource:** Access the Khan Academy *Algebra 1* course. Focus specifically on the modules for graphing lines.


* **Week 3 (Quadratics):** Quadratic functions (finding the vertex, slope, minima, and maxima), solving quadratic equations (roots and discriminant).
    * **Exact Resource:** Search for Professor Leonard on YouTube (Free). His intermediate algebra playlists break down straight lines and quadratics very clearly if you need slower, lecture-style explanations. Look for his specific videos on solving quadratic equations and finding the vertex.


* **Week 4 (Polynomial Algebra):** Adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing polynomials. Graphing polynomials (finding x-intercepts, multiplicity, end behavior, and turning points).
    * **Exact Resource:** Navigate to the Khan Academy *Algebra 2* course. Focus specifically on the modules for polynomials.

### 2. Statistics for Data Science I

This course starts from absolute zero. It focuses on descriptive statistics—how to organize, summarize, and visualize data.

* **Week 1 (Introduction):** Types of data (categorical vs. numerical), descriptive vs. inferential statistics, scales of measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio).
    * **Exact Resource:** Search for CrashCourse Statistics on YouTube (Free). Episodes 1 through 10 perfectly cover data types, central tendency, dispersion, and correlation in a highly visual way. Start with Episode 1 ("What Is Statistics") and Episode 2 ("Mathematical Thinking").


* **Week 2 (Describing Categorical Data):** Frequency distributions, creating and interpreting bar charts and pie charts, finding the mode and median for categorical variables.
    * **Exact Resource:** Watch CrashCourse Statistics Episode 5 ("Charts Are Like Pasta – Data Visualization Part 1").


* **Week 3 (Describing Numerical Data):** Measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode), quartiles and percentiles. Measures of spread (range, variance, standard deviation, Interquartile Range), and the five-number summary.
    * **Exact Resource:** Search StatQuest with Josh Starmer on YouTube (Free). Search for his videos on "Mean, Variance, and Standard Deviation". He explains the intuition behind the math brilliantly.


* **Week 4 (Association Between Variables):** Contingency tables (relative frequencies), scatterplots, covariance, Pearson correlation coefficient, and point-biserial correlation.
    * **Exact Resource:** Use StatQuest with Josh Starmer. Search for his videos on "Covariance and Correlation".

### 3. Computational Thinking (CT)

Crucial note: This course does not require you to write Python or code on a computer. It is entirely about algorithmic thinking using flowcharts and a specific "pseudocode" language created by IIT Madras. You will be doing manual, paper-and-pencil "dry runs" to trace how data moves through logic.

* **Week 1 (Basics):** Variables, initialization, iterators, filtering, data types, drawing flowcharts, and checking the sanity of data.
    * **Exact Resource:** Watch CS50x (Week 0) by Harvard on YouTube. This will teach you the logic of loops, conditionals, and variables before you even touch a specific programming language. Use Draw.io / Flowgorithm to practice taking everyday tasks (like making tea or finding the largest number in a list of 5) and drawing them as strict logical flowcharts.


* **Week 2 (Logic & Selection):** Iteration, filtering, selection constructs (if/else), finding maximum and minimum values, and the logical AND operator.
    * **Exact Resource:** Watch CS50x (Week 1) by Harvard on YouTube. Pay strict attention to the concepts of if/else statements and filtering data.


* **Week 3 (Procedures):** Multiple non-nested iterations, defining procedures and parameters, understanding side-effects, and the logical OR operator.
    * **Exact Resource:** Download Flowgorithm. Use it to visually build procedures and define parameters to see how logic flows step-by-step.


* **Week 4 (Nested Logic):** Nested iterations (loops inside loops), data binning/grouping, and solving probability problems like the Birthday Paradox using logic.
    * **Exact Resource:** Watch CrashCourse Statistics Episode 16 ("Geometric Distributions and The Birthday Paradox") to understand the probability math behind the Birthday Paradox logic. Practice drawing flowcharts with loops inside of loops using Draw.io.

### 4. English I

This is generally the easiest subject for most students, but it still requires attention to detail. It focuses on grammar, reading comprehension, and professional communication.

* **Week 1 to 3 (Sounds & Words):** Vocabulary building (synonyms, antonyms, word usage), understanding pronunciation basics, and recognizing grammatical roles.
    * **Exact Resource:** Use Daily Reading. Read articles on platforms like Medium or tech news sites to practice reading comprehension and picking up context. Supplement this by visiting British Council LearnEnglish to review grammatical roles.


* **Week 4 (Sentences):** Sentence correction, grammar fundamentals (prepositions, conjunctions, articles, tenses, subject-verb agreement), pronouns, and modifiers. The exams heavily feature reading comprehension passages with multiple-choice questions.
    * **Exact Resource:** Access the Grammarly Blog. Brush up on specific grammar rules like subject-verb agreement and modifier placement.
